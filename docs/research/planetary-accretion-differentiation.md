# Planetary Accretion and Differentiation: A Comprehensive Review

## 1. The Core Accretion Model -- How Rocky Planets Form from the Protoplanetary Disk

### 1.1 From Dust to Planetesimals

Planet formation begins in a protoplanetary disk of gas and dust orbiting a young star. The process is fundamentally bottom-up: micron-sized dust grains collide and stick together through van der Waals forces, gradually building larger aggregates. This initial coagulation phase produces millimeter- to centimeter-sized "pebbles" relatively quickly, but encounters a critical obstacle known as the **meter-size barrier** -- bodies approximately 1 meter in size experience aerodynamic drag from the surrounding gas that can spiral them into the star on timescales as short as ~100 years [Terrestrial planet formation (PNAS, 2011)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3228478/).

Two mechanisms overcome this barrier:

- **Streaming instability**: Solids concentrate in the gas when the local volume density of solids exceeds that of the gas (solid-to-gas ratio near or above 1), with optimal Stokes numbers of 0.01--3. Dense filaments of pebbles gravitationally collapse directly into ~100 km-scale planetesimals [Growth after the streaming instability (A&A, 2019)](https://www.aanda.org/articles/aa/full_html/2019/04/aa34174-18/aa34174-18.html).
- **Turbulent concentration**: In stagnant zones between turbulent eddies, solid-to-gas ratios can reach ~100, enabling rapid gravitational collapse [Terrestrial planet formation (PNAS, 2011)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3228478/).

The overall dust-to-planetesimal stage spans approximately **10^6 years (1 Myr)** [Terrestrial planet formation (PNAS, 2011)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3228478/).

### 1.2 Runaway and Oligarchic Growth

Once kilometer-scale planetesimals exist, gravitational focusing accelerates growth through two successive regimes:

1. **Runaway growth** (~10^4--10^5 years): The largest body in each local zone grows disproportionately fast because its increasing gravitational cross-section lets it sweep up smaller planetesimals at an ever-increasing rate. A single embryo dominates each annular feeding zone [Planetary formation and migration (Scholarpedia)](http://www.scholarpedia.org/article/Planetary_formation_and_migration).

2. **Oligarchic growth** (~10^6 years): As embryos grow, they stir up the velocity dispersion of surrounding planetesimals, reducing gravitational focusing. Growth rates equalize among the largest bodies. This phase produces **several tens to ~100 lunar- to Mars-mass embryos** (0.01--0.1 Earth masses) embedded in a swarm of remnant planetesimals [Terrestrial planet formation (PNAS, 2011)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3228478/).

3. **Giant impact phase** (10^7--10^8 years): Mutual gravitational perturbations among embryos lead to orbit-crossing and high-velocity collisions. Over 10--100 Myr, these collisions assemble the final terrestrial planets. Earth's Moon-forming giant impact is the archetype of this final stage [Terrestrial planet formation (PNAS, 2011)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3228478/).

### 1.3 Pebble Accretion

A more recently recognized mechanism, **pebble accretion**, can dramatically accelerate core growth. Millimeter- to centimeter-sized pebbles drifting inward through the disk are efficiently captured by existing planetesimals due to aerodynamic drag. Cores can grow to several Earth masses in as little as **~100,000 years** via this process, resolving earlier timescale problems for giant planet core formation [Core Accretion -- Planet Hunters](https://blog.planethunters.org/tag/core-accretion/); [From Streaming Instability to the Onset of Pebble Accretion (arXiv, 2025)](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.02124).

### 1.4 Summary of Formation Timescales

| Stage | Timescale | Product |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Dust coagulation to pebbles | ~10^3--10^4 yr | mm--cm grains |
| Pebbles to planetesimals (streaming instability) | ~10^5--10^6 yr | ~100 km bodies |
| Runaway growth | ~10^4--10^5 yr | Dominant embryo per zone |
| Oligarchic growth | ~10^6 yr | 10s--100s of lunar/Mars-mass embryos |
| Giant impacts / final assembly | 10^7--10^8 yr | Terrestrial planets |

## 2. The Iron Catastrophe: Core Formation and Mantle Differentiation

### 2.1 Mechanism

The **iron catastrophe** refers to the runaway process by which metallic iron-nickel alloy separates from silicate material and sinks to form a planetary core. As a growing planet accumulates mass, multiple heat sources raise its internal temperature past the melting point of iron (~1,538 degrees C / ~1,811 K):

- **Accretional energy**: Kinetic energy of impacting planetesimals converts to heat.
- **Short-lived radioactive isotopes**: Primarily **Aluminium-26** (half-life ~0.7 Myr), the main heat source for early protoplanet differentiation. Planetesimals larger than ~20 km radius accreting within 2 Myr of solar system formation receive sufficient Al-26 heating to melt [Magma ocean (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magma_ocean); [Planetary differentiation (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_differentiation).
- **Gravitational potential energy release**: Once iron begins sinking, the released gravitational energy further heats the interior in a positive feedback loop, making the process **self-reinforcing** ("catastrophic") [Iron catastrophe (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_catastrophe).

### 2.2 Mechanisms of Metal-Silicate Separation

Core formation proceeds through several physical mechanisms:

1. **Percolation**: Liquid metal migrates through a solid silicate matrix along grain boundaries. Timescale for complete separation: ~1--10 Myr [EBSCO Research: Earth's Differentiation](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/earth-and-atmospheric-sciences/earths-differentiation).
2. **Diapirism**: Large metal-rich blobs (diapirs) sink through partially molten silicate.
3. **Diking**: Metal-filled fractures propagate downward.
4. **Direct delivery**: Large impacts deliver pre-differentiated metallic cores directly to the growing planet's interior.

### 2.3 Magma Ocean and Core Segregation

Giant impacts create extensive **magma oceans** that enable rapid and efficient metal-silicate separation:

- **Earth's magma ocean**: Extended to depths of **770--1,600 km** after major impacts; the Moon-forming impact likely produced a magma ocean up to **2,000 km** deep [Magma oceans as a critical stage (Royal Society, 2018)](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2018.0109).
- **Metal-silicate equilibration pressure**: **30--40 GPa**, equivalent to depths of ~700 km in early Earth [The formation and differentiation of Earth (Physics Today)](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/the-formation-and-differentiation-of-earth).
- **Minimum pressure**: >25 GPa required to explain observed nickel and cobalt partitioning between Earth's core and mantle [The formation and differentiation of Earth (Physics Today)](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/the-formation-and-differentiation-of-earth).
- **Lunar magma ocean**: Initially 200--300 km thick, ~2,000 K; solidified ~80% by volume within the first ~1,000 years, but complete crystallization took 150--200 Myr [Magma oceans as a critical stage (Royal Society, 2018)](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2018.0109).

### 2.4 Earth's Resulting Layered Structure

The iron catastrophe produced Earth's fundamental layered structure:

| Layer | Depth | Density | Composition |
|-------|-------|---------|-------------|
| Crust | 5--50 km | ~2,700 kg/m^3 | Silicates, enriched in Al, U, K |
| Upper mantle | 50--650 km | ~3,400 kg/m^3 | Olivine, pyroxene |
| Lower mantle | 650--2,900 km | ~4,500--5,500 kg/m^3 | Mg- and Fe-bearing silicate perovskite |
| Outer core | 2,900--5,100 km | ~10,000--12,000 kg/m^3 | Liquid Fe-Ni + light elements (O, S, Si) |
| Inner core | 5,100--6,371 km | ~13,000 kg/m^3 | Solid Fe-Ni |

The core comprises **~32% of Earth's total mass** and contains **85--90% of Earth's iron**. More than 90% of Earth's mass is composed of iron, oxygen, silicon, and magnesium [Earth's Interior (Britannica)](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth/The-interior); [The formation and differentiation of Earth (Physics Today)](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/the-formation-and-differentiation-of-earth).

Core temperatures range from **4,000--5,000 K** at the outer core to **5,000--7,000 K** at Earth's center [Earth's Interior (Britannica)](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth/The-interior).

## 3. How Initial Composition Affects the Final Planet

### 3.1 Chondrite Classes as Planetary Building Blocks

Chondrites -- primitive, undifferentiated meteorites -- represent the raw materials from which terrestrial planets were assembled. They formed **4,566.6 +/- 1.0 Ma** ago and preserve a record of solar nebula conditions. Three major classes exist [Chondrite (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chondrite):

**Carbonaceous chondrites (C chondrites):**
- Comprise <5% of chondrite falls
- Carbon content up to **3%** (as graphite, carbonates, organic compounds including amino acids)
- Contain significant water and hydrated minerals
- **CI chondrites** most closely match solar photosphere composition (excluding H and He), with Mg/Si ratio of **1.07** matching solar values
- Lack metallic iron; highly oxidized
- Formed at larger distances from the Sun where temperatures were lower
- [Carbonaceous chondrite (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite); [EBSCO Research: Carbonaceous Chondrites](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/astronomy-and-astrophysics/carbonaceous-chondrites)

**Ordinary chondrites (O chondrites):**
- Comprise ~80% of all meteorites, >90% of chondrites
- Three subtypes by iron content:
  - **H chondrites**: 15--20% Fe-Ni metal by mass
  - **L chondrites**: 7--11% Fe-Ni metal by mass
  - **LL chondrites**: 3--5% Fe-Ni metal by mass
- Matrix content: 10--15% of the rock
- [Chondrite (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chondrite)

**Enstatite chondrites (E chondrites):**
- Comprise ~2% of chondrite falls
- Iron predominantly in **metallic or sulfide** state (not oxides) -- formed under highly reducing conditions
- Display the **least isotopic differences** relative to Bulk Silicate Earth for oxygen, titanium, calcium, chromium, molybdenum, nitrogen, and hydrogen isotopes
- Strong genetic link to Earth's composition at the ppm--ppb level
- [Si-Mg isotopes in enstatite chondrites (Nature Scientific Reports, 2020)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-57635-1); [Enstatite Chondrite (ScienceDirect Topics)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/earth-and-planetary-sciences/enstatite-chondrite)

### 3.2 Compositional Consequences for Planet Formation

The choice of building-block composition profoundly affects the resulting planet:

- **A planet built primarily from enstatite chondrite material** would have a larger metallic core (more metallic iron available) and a more reduced mantle. Earth's isotopic fingerprint most closely matches enstatite chondrites, suggesting these were the dominant building blocks, though the chemical match is imperfect [Porphyritic olivine chondrules with enstatite chondrite isotopic composition (Earth & Planetary Science Letters, 2025)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0012821X25001360).

- **A planet built from carbonaceous chondrites** would be volatile-rich: more water, more carbon compounds, but with a smaller metallic core (less metallic iron). The silicate mantle would be more oxidized.

- **Earth's actual composition** appears to reflect a mixture, with progressive oxidation during accretion. Earth began as a chemically reduced body (enstatite-like) and accreted increasingly oxidized, volatile-rich material from expanding feeding zones [The formation and differentiation of Earth (Physics Today)](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/the-formation-and-differentiation-of-earth).

- Earth's silicate mantle retains **chondritic (solar) relative proportions** of refractory lithophile elements (Ca, Al, Sc, Ti, rare earths) but shows **progressive depletion of volatile elements** relative to CI chondrite reference standards [The formation and differentiation of Earth (Physics Today)](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/the-formation-and-differentiation-of-earth).

## 4. Timescales for Differentiation

### 4.1 The Hf-W Chronometer

The extinct **182-Hf to 182-W** decay system (half-life = **9 Myr**) is the premier tool for dating core formation. Hafnium is lithophile (prefers silicates) while tungsten is siderophile (prefers metal). When metal separates from silicate during core formation, Hf remains in the mantle while W partitions into the core. The resulting excess 182-W in the mantle (from 182-Hf decay after separation) timestamps the event [A short timescale for terrestrial planet formation from Hf-W chronometry (Nature, 2002)](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature00995); [Rapid accretion and early core formation (Nature, 2002)](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature00982).

### 4.2 Comparative Differentiation Timescales

| Body | Core Formation (after T_0) | Notes |
|------|---------------------------|-------|
| Small asteroids (e.g., Vesta) | ~2 Myr | Earliest differentiated bodies; Al-26 heated |
| Mars | ~7 Myr | Rapid differentiation; magma ocean crystallized in <5--10 Myr |
| Earth | ~30 Myr (bulk) | Exponential growth tau ~10--11 Myr; 63% mass by 11 Myr |
| Moon | ~60--150 Myr after T_0 | Formed from Earth's mantle after giant impact |

Sources: [The formation and differentiation of Earth (Physics Today)](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/the-formation-and-differentiation-of-earth); [The early differentiation of Mars (Earth & Planetary Science Letters, 2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0012821X17303710); [Magma oceans as a critical stage (Royal Society, 2018)](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2018.0109).

### 4.3 Detailed Earth Chronology

The solar system age is precisely dated at **4,568 +/- 0.001 Gyr** (from CAI -- calcium-aluminium-rich inclusion -- dating). Earth's accretion and differentiation timeline:

- **T_0 (4,568 Ma)**: First solids (CAIs) condense in solar nebula
- **~2 Myr**: Chondrules form; earliest planetesimals differentiate (driven by Al-26)
- **~11 Myr**: Earth reached ~63% of final mass (exponential growth constant tau = 10--11 Myr)
- **~30 Myr**: Earth reached ~93% of final mass; bulk core formation essentially complete (182-W/184-W evidence)
- **~50--100 Myr**: Moon-forming giant impact; final ~10% of mass added as "late veneer"
- **~150--200 Myr**: Lunar magma ocean fully crystallized

[The formation and differentiation of Earth (Physics Today)](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/the-formation-and-differentiation-of-earth)

### 4.4 Mars: A Faster Clock

Mars differentiated significantly faster than Earth:
- Core formation within **~7 Myr** of T_0
- Magma ocean crystallization completed within **20--25 Myr**
- Crust formation ~15 Myr after magma ocean solidification (i.e., ~35--40 Myr after T_0)
- Major differentiation into mantle, crust, and atmosphere occurred between **20 and 40 Myr** after solar system formation

[The early differentiation of Mars (Earth & Planetary Science Letters, 2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0012821X17303710); [Magma oceans as a critical stage (Royal Society, 2018)](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2018.0109).

## 5. What Determines Final Planetary Composition

### 5.1 The Condensation Sequence and Distance from the Star

The protoplanetary disk exhibits a strong radial temperature gradient -- hot near the star, cold at large distances. This gradient creates a **condensation sequence** that fundamentally controls which materials are available in solid form at each orbital distance:

| Material | Condensation Temperature | Approximate Distance (Solar Nebula) |
|----------|------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Refractory oxides (Al2O3, CaO) | ~1,700 K | <0.5 AU |
| Metallic iron, nickel | ~1,400 K | ~0.5--1 AU |
| Silicates (Mg-silicates) | ~1,300 K | ~0.7 AU (midplane) |
| Water ice (snow line) | ~150--170 K | ~2.7--5 AU |
| CO, N2 ices | ~20--30 K | >30 AU |

Sources: [Frost line (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics)); [Fossilized condensation lines in the Solar System (Icarus, 2016)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0019103515005448); [Maximum temperatures in evolving protoplanetary discs (MNRAS, 2021)](https://academic.oup.com/mnras/article/503/4/5254/6188378).

### 5.2 The Snow Line: The Critical Boundary

The **frost line** (or snow line) is the most consequential boundary in the disk:

- Located at approximately **2.7 AU** for water ice (at ~170 K) based on Hayashi (1981), though estimates range from **3.0--3.2 AU** (143--150 K) in more recent models
- During early disk evolution, the snow line may have been as far as **~5 AU** and its maximum radius can reach **17.4 AU** for a solar-mass star before migrating inward
- Ceres orbits at 2.77 AU, almost exactly at the lower snow line estimate, consistent with its water-ice-bearing composition

[Frost line (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics)); [Planets and the Snow Line (Harvard CfA)](https://www.cfa.harvard.edu/news/planets-and-snow-line); [Variable snow lines affect planet formation (Nature, 2016)](https://www.nature.com/articles/535237a).

**Inside the snow line**: Only refractory materials (metals, silicates) condense. Planets are metal-and-rock dominated with limited volatiles -- this is why Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars are small, dense, rocky worlds.

**Beyond the snow line**: Water ice and other volatiles condense, roughly tripling the available solid mass. This enables rapid growth to the ~10 Earth-mass critical core threshold needed to initiate runaway gas accretion, explaining why Jupiter and Saturn formed as gas giants [Why do the solar system's planets have different compositions? (Astronomy.com)](https://www.astronomy.com/science/why-do-the-solar-systems-planets-have-different-compositions/).

### 5.3 Volatile Inventory

A planet's volatile budget depends on multiple factors:

1. **Formation location**: Distance from star sets baseline volatile availability.
2. **Feeding zone width**: During the giant impact phase, embryos scatter and mix material from a range of heliocentric distances. Earth likely accreted material from ~0.5--4 AU, progressively sampling more volatile-rich (oxidized) material over time [The formation and differentiation of Earth (Physics Today)](https://physicstoday.aip.org/features/the-formation-and-differentiation-of-earth).
3. **Late veneer / late accretion**: After core formation was largely complete, a final ~0.5--1% of Earth's mass was added as a "late veneer" of volatile-rich material, delivering siderophile elements (Pt, Ir, Os) and possibly significant water and carbon.
4. **Impact erosion**: Large impacts can strip atmospheres and volatiles, depleting the volatile inventory.
5. **Magma ocean degassing**: Volatiles dissolved in the magma ocean are released during crystallization, building the early atmosphere and hydrosphere.

### 5.4 The "Soot Line"

Beyond the traditional snow line concept, research has identified additional compositional boundaries. The **soot line** at ~0.1 AU marks where carbon-rich refractory organics are destroyed by high temperatures, potentially affecting the carbon budget of close-in planets [A New Place to Search for Habitable Planets: The Soot Line (Universe Today)](https://www.universetoday.com/articles/a-new-place-to-search-for-habitable-planets-the-soot-line).
